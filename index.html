Excessive Use of Fertilizers

Fertilizers containing nitrogen and phosphorus are often applied in amounts far beyond what plants need. Excess nutrients enter water systems through runoff, triggering algal blooms and eutrophication — a growing threat to ecosystems and sustainable food production.

Slow-Release Fertilizers

Instead of flooding soil with nutrients all at once, slow-release fertilizers deliver nutrients gradually over time — allowing plants to absorb what they need, when they need it, and dramatically reducing environmental harm.

How it works

1

Controlled coating

Fertilizer granules are coated with polymer or sulfur layers that regulate how quickly nutrients dissolve into the soil.

2

Gradual nutrient release

Nutrients are released slowly over weeks or months, matched to the plant's natural growth cycle rather than all at once.

3

Efficient plant uptake

Plants absorb nutrients more efficiently because supply matches demand — less is wasted and less escapes into waterways.

4

Reduced runoff

With fewer excess nutrients in the soil at any one time, the risk of nitrogen and phosphorus leaching into water systems is significantly lowered.

Key benefits

Less water pollution

Reduced nutrient runoff means fewer algal blooms and less eutrophication in rivers, lakes, and oceans.

Better plant health

A steady nutrient supply promotes stronger, more consistent plant growth compared to irregular large doses.

Efficient resource use

Less fertilizer is wasted, meaning farmers can use smaller quantities while achieving the same or better crop yields.

Supports sustainability

Aligns with global goals for responsible production by reducing the environmental footprint of farming.

Limitations to consider

Drawbacks of Slow-Release Fertilizers

While slow-release fertilizers offer real environmental benefits, they are not without their challenges. Understanding these limitations is key to making informed decisions about their use.

Key limitations

Usually more expensive than conventional fertilizers
The coating technology and manufacturing process makes slow-release fertilizers significantly costlier upfront. For small-scale or subsistence farmers, this price gap can be a real barrier to adoption, even if long-term savings offset the initial cost.
Nutrient release rate depends on temperature and soil conditions
Release rates are influenced by soil temperature, moisture, and microbial activity. In cooler climates or dry conditions, nutrients may be released too slowly. In hot, wet conditions, they may release faster than intended — making results harder to predict.
May not supply nutrients fast enough in urgent situations
If crops show signs of nutrient deficiency mid-season, slow-release fertilizers cannot correct the problem quickly. Farmers may still need to apply a fast-acting supplement alongside them, which adds cost and complexity.
Some coatings use synthetic materials that create waste concerns
Polymer-coated fertilizers can leave microplastic residues in the soil after the coating breaks down. While biodegradable coatings exist, they are not yet universally adopted — meaning some products may contribute to long-term soil contamination.
Farmers may need new application methods or management practices
Switching to slow-release fertilizers often requires adjusting application timing, rates, and equipment. Without proper training or guidance, farmers risk under- or over-applying them, which could reduce effectiveness and negate the environmental benefits.
